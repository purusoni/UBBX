Term Project 2026: Decentralized Marketplace: Building a
Web3 Economy on Ethereum Version 1
Copyright 2026 Bina Ramamurthy
February 2, 2026
Abstract
This project challenges students to design, develop, and deploy a decentralized autonomous
marketplace on the Ethereum Sepolia testnet. The platform will enable users to buy and sell prod-
ucts and services in a peer-to-peer web3 environment, with all transactions secured and recorded on
the blockchain. Participants will create and list their own products or services—anything that can
be delivered safely, securely, and legally. The entire class community, including students, instruc-
tor, TA, and volunteer staff, will participate in this functioning economy, transacting with each
other as both buyers and sellers. The marketplace will operate using a custom currency system
built on fungible tokens. Our monetary unit, the Yoda token, will serve as the medium of exchange
for all purchases and peer-to-peer transfers within the platform. Beyond building the marketplace,
students will analyze system performance by measuring transaction throughput (transaction rate)
and average transaction fees. This hands-on experience will provide practical insight into the
capabilities and constraints of decentralized applications. The project culminates in a working au-
tonomous marketplace where participants can freely join and leave, with the Ethereum blockchain
serving as the trust layer that enables decentralized commerce and digital self-sovereignty.
1 Problem Definition
Figure 1 presents a use case diagram: a standard tool for defining a system’s problem statement at a
high level. Five actors interact with YodaMart, the decentralized peer-to-peer marketplace (feel free
to choose a different name for your implementation). These five roles are:
• Role 1: Treasurer or the monetary system manager The treasurer funds the project with
a limited amount of money (value) in the form of tokens called Yoda, a fungible token (FT).
• Role 2: Project Manager and Assistant Treasurer The Treasurer establishes and funds
the marketplace economy with a limited supply of Yoda tokens, the platform’s fungible token
(FT) currency.
• Role 3: Decentralized Participants Participants form the core community of the market-
place. They receive an initial allocation of Yodas from the Treasurer and periodic incentive
distributions from the Project Manager. From this base role, two specialized roles emerge:
– Seller (specialized participant role): Sellers define products, services, and digital assets.
Each seller develops a web interface where buyers can connect their wallets and purchase
listed products. A seller will support a web interface for participants to connect their wallets
and purchase the products they sell.
– Buyer (specialized participant role): Buyers connect to seller interfaces, browse available
products, and complete purchases by transferring the required Yodas to the seller.
1.1 Limitations
Each seller is limited to a single product line or genre, though they may sell multiple items within that
category. For example, a seller offering artwork can sell numbered editions of that piece but cannot
expand into other product types; they cannot sell artwork and fresh vegetables (haha), for example.

This constraint keeps the focus on the web3 system architecture rather than on building complex

Products can be anything of value to participants: physical objects, services, collectibles, or in-
tangible digital goods. Sellers must be able to deliver their products to buyers through appropriate
means: electronically, by mail, or in person.
2 Scenario and Operational Details
The following steps describe the operation of the YodaMart platform.
1. Yoda Deployment: Bina deploys the Yoda fungible token (FT) with a total supply of 1,000,000
Yodas.
2. Initial Distribution: Bina grants 250,000 Yodas to TA Jakir as a grant to distribute to the
students as rewards for various activities.
3. Participant Onboarding: Students join the platform by connecting their wallets and receive
an initial allocation of 10,000 Yodas each.
4. Product Definition: Each team selects a single product to design and develop as a digital asset
for sale. Products should be realistic, useful, and desirable to other participants and potential
buyers outside the class. Teams advertise their products on their web interface.
5. Production: Each team creates multiple instances of their assets and lists them for sale at a
base price (e.g., 100 chickens at 20 Yodas each).
6. Purchase Process: Buyers visit a seller’s web interface, connect their wallet, and transfer the
required Yodas to purchase items offered.
7. Item Transfer: Upon payment, the seller transfers a unique product item (asset) instance to
the buyer.
8. Incentive Activities: Periodically, Bina or TA Jakir will post activities or contests with Yoda
rewards. These may be on-chain or off-chain tasks. Teams or individuals who complete them
earn additional Yodas.
9. Marketplace Launch: Once a team’s product and web interface are ready, they can advertise
on Piazza for the class community to browse and buy.
2
10. Ongoing Operations: Steps 6–9 repeat throughout the semester as the marketplace economy
evolves.
11. Performance Analysis: After the experiment concludes, teams extract transaction data from
Sepolia to assess performance metrics: number of transactions, average transaction fees, through-
put, and other relevant measures.
2.1 Tokenization of Assets and Products
Ethereum provides standard protocols for tokenizing assets: ERC-20 for fungible tokens and ERC-721
for non-fungible tokens. We will use the ERC-20 token standard to implement the Yoda currency
(FT) and, optionally, the ERC-721 standard to implement the digital assets representing each team’s
product.
2.2 Idea Requirements
Here are some idea requirements:
1. Useful to Society: Your fellow students are your primary customers—design something valu-
able to your community.
2. Transferable and Tradeable: The asset should be sellable or exchanged for cryptocurrencies.
3. Self-Sovereign Ownership: Like an automobile or a plane ticket, the owner has full control—self-
owned, self-managed, and independent of intermediaries.
4. Complex Off-Chain Data: The asset may represent a complex object with data stored off-
chain, as on-chain storage is limited.
5. Any Domain: Products can span any field—mathematics, chemistry, physics, automotive, real
estate, services, finance, healthcare, astronomy, space, civil engineering, education, social science,
sports, medicine, culture, politics, music, travel, employment, and beyond.

What are events?
• Events are notifications that can be emitted from
functions to indicate the presence of a condition or flag
during a smart contract function’s execution.
• Solidity provides features to define and emit an event
with and without parameters.
• Events are logged on-chain, in the receipt tree, and can
be accessed by their names.
• Of course, events represent application-specific
“events” or “situations” that may need participants
attention and actions.
Bina Ramamurthy. Copyright 2020
How to define an event?
• Here is the syntax for an event definition:
event NameOfEvent (parameters);
• Event names in Solidity begin with an uppercase letter and then use camel case.
There can be three parameters at most.
• This limitation is set by Solidity to avoid overloading the chain and to enable efficient
management of the event logs. Here’s an example of an event definition for the blind
auction smart contract introduced in chapter 5:
event AuctionEnded(address winner, uint highestBid);
• You can also define events without any parameters by specifying them for different
phases of the blind auction (here, Bidding and Reveal):
event BiddingStarted();
event RevealStarted();
Bina Ramamurthy. Copyright 2020
Where do you define an event in an
SC?
• You can define an event anywhere in the smart contract
code before you use it.
• Still, it’s a good idea to set aside a standard location to
define the events in your design and coding so that they
can be easily identified during the development and
code review process.
• You can define events in the smart contract right after
the type and variable declarations.
